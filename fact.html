What is Coral Reef

Corals are invertebrate animal species which belong to a large group of colourful and fascinating animals called Cnidaria. Each individual coral is called a polyp which live mostly in groups of hundreds to thousands of genetically identical polyps that eventually form a 'colony'.

Coral are generally classified as either "hard coral" or "soft coral". There are around 800 known species of hard coral which are also known as the 'reef building' corals. Soft corals, which include seas fans, sea feathers and sea whips, are different from hard coral as they grow wood-like cores for support and fleshy rinds for protection.

Coral reefs are the largest living structure on Earth and is the only living structure to be visible from space.

Why it is important

• For Coastal Protection:

Coral reefs are a very effective for absorbing elements coming from the ocean, they absorb waves energy and contribute to environmental protection through the reduction of coastal erosion. They reduce the damage in case of storms, hurricanes, and in some way, the energy of tsunamis. Therefore, they protect both ecosystems located between the reefs and coasts and human settlements located by the sea.

• For Habitat:

Over one million plant and animal species are involved in coral reefs and they hostreceive more than 25% of all species of marine life. It is one of the most important biodiversity hotspot on earth.

• For Food Resource:

One eighth of the world’s population, approximately 850 million people live within 100 kilometres of coral and a large proportion of food taken directly from the reef waters.

Threats

Most coral reefs occur in shallow water near shore. Regardless of global threats to coral reef such as ocean acidification, increased ocean temperatures and changing ocean chemistry. Coral reefs are particularly vulnerable to the effects of human activities, both through direct exploitation of reef resources, and through indirect impacts from adjacent human activities on land and in the coastal zone.



• Physical damage or destruction from coastal development
• Pollution from land (Main reason)
- Sedimentation from coastal development
- Pathogens from inadequately treated sewage
- Toxic substances, including metals, organic chemicals and pesticides found in industrial discharges, sunscreens, etc.
- Trash and micro-plastics from improper disposal, such as plastic bags and bottles which can snag on corals and block the sunlight needed for photosynthesis.
• Overfishing
• Coral harvesting
